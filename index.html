An EPHA2-centered gene regulatory network associates with hyperbaric oxygen treatment response in perianal fistulizing Crohn’s disease patients

  • Get Started

Introduction

Perianal fistulizing Crohn’s disease (PFCD) is a debilitating CD complication characterized by the formation of a tract (fistula) between the anorectal mucosa and other epithelial-lined surfaces. Despite the standard multidisciplinary approach combining surgery and medical management, long-term remission rates remain low, often not exceeding 50%1. Hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBOT) has shown benefits in PFCD as an adjunct therapy following surgical closure2. Our aim is to evaluate its mechanism for enhancing long-term remission in PFCD patients following surgery.

Aim

We aim to investigate the cellular representation and transcriptomic processes in fistula biopsies before and after HBOT through single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) and assess the differences between remitters and non-remitters.

Method

Patients with active PFCD refractory to optimized medical therapy were included and treated with 40 sessions of HBOT (110 minutes with 5-minute air breaks, 100% oxygen at 2.4 atmosphere) (Fig. 1).

Biopsies were collected from the internal opening and adjacent rectal mucosa at baseline and 4 weeks into HBOT, which fistula closure surgery was performed after. Biopsies were subsequently analyzed with scRNA-seq to find differences between remitters and non-remitters.
Remission was defined as combined clinical (no draining openings) and MRI remission (MAGNIFI-CD ≤ 6) at 26 weeks follow-up.

Figure 1: Timeline of the HBOT study design

Results

We included 10 patients (7 remitters and 3 non-remitters), from which we acquired transcriptomic data of 34,185 cells (Fig. 2A and 2B). At baseline, remitter-derived epithelial cells displayed a gene-regulatory network (GRN) in epithelial cells centered around EPHA2 and its downstream ephrin pathway, which disappeared at follow-up (Fig. 2C). Notably, the EPHA2 driven GRN was observable among remitters but not non-remitters prior to the start of treatment (Fig. 2D) whilst EPHA2 expression significantly decreased in remitters, it was unaffected in non-remitters over time (Fig. 2E). The EPHA2-GRN showed no significant differential expression when comparing inflamed and non-inflamed colonic biopsies from Kong et al.3, suggesting EPHA2 GRN is not related to an inflammation-dependent process (Fig. 2F).
